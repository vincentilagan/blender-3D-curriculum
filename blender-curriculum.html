Blender 3D in the Curriculum

Presentation guide for a school proposal

Proposal Presentation

Blender 3D as part of the school curriculum

This proposal introduces students to 3D modeling, animation, design thinking, problem solving, and digital creativity using a free, powerful, and industry-recognized software.

Opening line: “Good day. This proposal shows how Blender 3D can make learning more practical, creative, and future-ready for students.”
Why Blender?

Free, powerful, and used across creative industries

Accessible Blender is free to use, which makes it practical for schools and students.
Skill-based Students learn modeling, animation, lighting, rendering, and visual storytelling.
Career-ready It connects to animation, game design, architecture, product design, and media arts.
Presentation tip: Emphasize that this is not just “drawing on a computer.” It develops creativity, logic, planning, and technical skills.
Learning Goals

What will students learn?

  • Basic 3D navigation, shapes, objects, and workspace tools.
  • How to create simple 3D objects such as a room, product, character, or logo.
  • Materials, colors, lighting, camera angles, and rendering.
  • Simple animation using keyframes.
  • A portfolio project that can be presented in a school exhibit or performance task.
Curriculum Integration

How can it fit into school subjects?

TLE / ICT 3D modeling, digital production, file management, and project output.
Arts Form, color, composition, character design, and visual storytelling.
Math / STEM Geometry, measurement, symmetry, scale, spatial thinking, and problem solving.

Blender can be offered as a module, elective, club activity, or project-based unit depending on grade level and computer lab availability.

Sample 6-Week Plan

Simple implementation timeline

Week 1
Introduction to the Blender interface, controls, shapes, and basic navigation.
Week 2
Basic modeling: create a desk, chair, simple room, or school-related object.
Week 3
Materials, colors, texture basics, lighting, and camera setup.
Week 4
Animation basics: movement, rotation, keyframes, and a short scene.
Week 5-6
Final project, peer review, rendering, and class presentation.
Assessment

How can student output be graded?

Technical Skill The model, scale, objects, lighting, and render quality are clear and organized.
Creativity The concept is original and the visual idea is communicated well.
Process The student shows planning, progress screenshots, reflection, and final presentation.
Suggested grading: 40% final output, 30% process, 20% creativity, 10% presentation.
Resources Needed

What is needed to get started?

  • Computer lab or shared devices with Blender installed.
  • A mouse with a scroll wheel for easier 3D navigation.
  • Teacher orientation or a short training session.
  • Prepared modules, sample projects, and grading rubric.
  • Final showcase through a classroom exhibit, digital gallery, or video presentation.
How To Present

Suggested presentation flow

  • Start with the need: students need practical digital and creative skills.
  • Introduce Blender as a free and powerful learning tool.
  • Explain which subjects Blender can support.
  • Show the sample 6-week plan and assessment rubric.
  • End with the benefits: creativity, confidence, portfolio building, and career readiness.
Closing line: “With Blender 3D, students do not only learn software. They learn how to think, design, plan, and create outputs with real-world value.”
Closing

Future-ready learning starts with creative tools.

Blender 3D is a practical way to combine technology, arts, STEM, and project-based learning into one engaging curriculum experience.
